# Double-Loop Learning

## Overview

Double-Loop Learning is a concept proposed by Chris Argyris and others. In contrast to **"Single-Loop Learning,"** which corrects actions according to existing goals and rules, Double-Loop Learning is a form of learning that questions those goals and rules (values and assumptions) themselves to encourage fundamental transformation. It aims not just to improve "how things are done" but to update the **"Framework"** of "why we do them" in the first place.

## Rating (1–5)

- **Applicability:** 5
- **Effectiveness:** 5
- **Complexity:** 4
- **Misuse Risk:** 3

### Evaluation Comment

This perspective is indispensable for self-transformation and organizational innovation in uncertain environments. However, because it requires denying one's own beliefs and past success stories, psychological resistance is strong, and the difficulty of practice is extremely high.

---

## The First Question

> **"Am I merely trying to optimize within the current rules, or am I able to suspect that the rules themselves might be outdated?"**

### Objectives

- To break away from superficial problem-solving (symptomatic treatment) and resolve root causes.
- To timely update one's own **"Mental Models"** in rapidly changing environments.
- To prevent organizations and individuals from falling into a "learning-disabled" state (rigidity).

### Poor Questions

- "What should I do next time so I don't fail?" (Stuck in correcting methods = Single-Loop.)
- "Who decided this rule?" (Leads to shifting blame rather than questioning the assumptions.)
- "How can we increase efficiency?" (Ignoring the possibility that the objective itself is wrong.)

---

## How to Use (Step-by-Step)

1. **Acknowledge the Mismatch in Results**
   Face the reality where expected results are not being achieved, or where results are achieved but feel "off."
2. **Identify the "Assumptions" Behind the "Methods"**
   Verbalize the rationale that led to the choice of action (e.g., industry common sense, past successes, personal beliefs).
3. **"Destroy" and "Reconstruct" the Assumptions**
   Hypothesize "What if that assumption was wrong?" and redefine the action plan based on new objectives or rules.

---

## Output Examples

- **Shift in Sales Strategy:**
  Instead of "increasing the number of visits (Action Correction)," question "whether face-to-face sales are necessary in the first place (Rethinking Assumptions)" and transition to a digital-marketing-centered model.
- **The State of Meetings:**
  Instead of "shortening meeting times," redefine the purpose itself—"what decision is this meeting for and who needs to be there"—and abolish unnecessary meetings entirely.

---

## Use Cases

- **Business:** Pivoting a business, refreshing organizational culture, and radically reviewing existing processes.
- **Daily Life:** When a habit doesn't stick, return to the motivation of "why do I want to do this?" instead of relying on willpower.
- **Decision Making / Thinking:** When feeling a project has hit a wall, verify whether past "success patterns" have become a hindrance.

## Typical Misuses

- **Neglecting Single-Loop Learning:** Routine improvements (Single-Loop) are also important; questioning assumptions for everything can lead to organizational chaos and decreased execution power.
- **Superficial "Questioning of Assumptions":** Swapping out only convenient assumptions while hiding beliefs you don't actually want to change, resulting in "pretend transformation."
- **Lack of Psychological Safety:** In an organization where questioning assumptions is seen as "rebellion," Double-Loop Learning will not function.

## Relationship with Other Models

- **Complementary:** First-Principles Thinking (deconstructing assumptions to the limit), Ladder of Inference (visualizing one's own interpretation process).
- **Related:** Mental Models, Unlearning, Backcasting.
