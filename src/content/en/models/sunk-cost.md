# Sunk Cost

## Overview

A Sunk Cost is a cost that has already been invested and cannot be recovered. In rational decision-making, only future costs and benefits should be considered. However, humans are often trapped by the psychological bias known as the **"Sunk Cost Fallacy"** — the feeling that **"I’ve already invested this much, so I can't stop now"** — leading them to persist in a losing course of action.

## Rating (1–5)

- **Applicability:** 5
- **Effectiveness:** 5
- **Complexity:** 2
- **Misuse Risk:** 4

### Evaluation Comment

This is a powerful bias that appears in everything from business investment decisions to personal relationships. Simply understanding this concept and learning to judge from a **"Zero-Base"** perspective can drastically reduce wasted losses. However, care must be taken to distinguish between "perseverance for mastery" and "persistence due to sunk costs."

---

## The First Question

> **"If I encountered this situation for the first time today, would I choose to invest in this project (or relationship) all over again?"**

### Objectives

- To let go of past attachments and make decisions that maximize **"Future Benefits."**
- To detach from unrecoverable losses and reallocate limited resources to more promising targets.
- To justify an early exit from failing projects (The Concorde Effect).

### Poor Questions

- "How much have we spent so far?" (Past expenditures cannot serve as a criterion for future judgment.)
- "If I quit now, won't all my previous efforts be wasted?" (Past efforts won't return even if you continue. Continuing risks wasting even more of the future.)
- "Shouldn't I at least try to break even?" (The idea of "breaking even" is itself a trap of the sunk cost mindset.)

---

## How to Use (Step-by-Step)

1. **Identify Unrecoverable Costs**
   Exclude already paid money, elapsed time, and spent emotions from your calculations as if they "never happened."
2. **Compare Future Alternatives**
   Compare only the future net benefits of "quitting now" versus "continuing with additional investment from this point forward."
3. **Consider "Opportunity Cost"**
   Maintain a perspective on how much value those resources (time or money) could generate if used for something else, then make the decision to withdraw.

---

## Output Examples

- **Withdrawal from Unprofitable Projects:**
  If a system that cost 100 million yen to develop is found to be unprofitable upon completion, do not finish it just to justify the 100 million yen. Stop development immediately and allocate the remaining budget to a different venture.
- **Partially Read Books:**
  Stop reading a boring book even if you are "halfway through," and spend that time on a new book (more valuable information).

---

## Use Cases

- **Business:** Judging the continuation of R&D, closing unprofitable stores, and canceling marketing campaigns.
- **Daily Life:** Canceling a gym membership or lessons that no longer fit, deciding whether to keep waiting in a long line, and disposing of old clothes or unused items.
- **Decision Making / Thinking:** When holding onto a losing stock investment simply because selling it feels like "wasting" the initial purchase price.

## Typical Misuses

- **Ignoring the "Value of Persistence":** Accumulated experience or the building of trust are not sunk costs if they contribute to "future benefits." These should still be considered.
- **Tool for Evading Responsibility:** Using the excuse "it’s a sunk cost, don't worry about it" to justify losses that could have been prevented, effectively hiding one's own failure.
- **Disregarding Emotional Costs:** Even if logic dictates withdrawal, humans have emotions. If the mental damage of a sudden cut is too great, the withdrawal process may need to be handled more gradually.

## Relationship with Other Models

- **Complementary:** Opportunity Cost (the benefit lost by not choosing the next best alternative), Loss Aversion (the psychological tendency to fear loss more than gain).
- **Related:** Concorde Effect, Status Quo Bias, Escalation of Commitment.
- **Opposing:** "Persistence pays off" (which can lead into the sunk cost trap if applied in the wrong context).
