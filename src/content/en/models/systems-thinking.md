# Systems Thinking

## Overview
A framework for seeing interrelationships rather than things, and for seeing patterns of change rather than static snapshots. It shifts the focus from linear cause-and-effect to understanding the **「circular structures」** and feedback loops that drive complex systems.

## Evaluation (1–5)
**Versatility**:: 5  
**Immediacy**:: 2  
**Difficulty**:: 5  
**Misuse Risk**:: 4  

### Editorial Comments
**Comment**:: Extremely effective for tackling complex, wicked problems. However, if the abstraction becomes too high, there is a danger that the model will fail to translate into **「actionable steps」**.

## The First Question
- "Within what kind of **「structure」** is this phenomenon occurring?"

### Objectives
- Avoid treating events as isolated incidents.
- Maintain awareness of the cycle between cause and effect.
- Prevent local optimization (improving one part at the expense of the whole).

### Poor Questions
- "Who is to blame?"
- "Which single part should we fix to solve this?"

## How to Use (Mini-Steps)
1. **List** the elements that constitute the problem.  
2. **Connect** the elements with arrows to show causal relationships.  
3. **Identify** reinforcing loops (positive feedback) and balancing loops (negative feedback).  
4. **Determine** the leverage points where intervention would be most effective.

## Use Cases
- **Business**: Organizational issues, customer churn, and business stagnation.  
- **Daily Life**: Habit formation, health management, and interpersonal relationships.  
- **Decision Making**: When a problem occurs repeatedly despite previous "fixes."

## Common Misuses
- Making everything unnecessarily complex.
- Letting the act of **「drawing the diagram」** become the goal itself.
- Failing to identify specific, executable intervention points.
- Drawing causal links based on assumptions rather than evidence.

## Relationship with Other Models
- **Related**: Second-Order Thinking, Causal Thinking.  
- **Complementary**: First Principles Thinking (decomposition), Theory of Constraints (identifying bottlenecks).  
- **Contrast**: Linear Thinking, Blame-Seeking Thinking.
