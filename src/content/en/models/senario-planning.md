# Scenario Planning

## Overview
A strategic method used to manage future uncertainty by developing multiple, plausible future scenarios. Rather than trying to predict a single outcome, this model focuses on structuring possible futures to prepare adaptable strategies and decision criteria.

## Evaluation (1–5)
**Versatility**:: 5  
**Immediacy**:: 3  
**Difficulty**:: 4  
**Misuse Risk**:: 3  

### Editorial Comments
**Comment**:: A high-level model for addressing uncertainty. By structuring **「future visions」** as frameworks rather than predictions, it broadens your range of options. However, there is a risk of becoming satisfied with just **「drawing the maps」** without taking action.

## The First Question
- "How might this future change based on different underlying assumptions?"

### Objectives
- Visualize future uncertainties.
- Enhance the capacity to respond to the **「unexpected」**.

### Pitfall Questions
- "Can we predict the single correct future?"
- "What if our forecast is wrong?" (Fear of inaccuracy).

## How to Use (Mini-Steps)
1. **Identify** key drivers (influential factors) affecting the future.  
2. **Define** scenario axes based on the most critical uncertainties.  
3. **Draft** 2 to 4 plausible future scenarios.  
4. **Determine** guidelines and strategic responses for each scenario.

## Output Examples
- **Bullet Points:**
  - Scenario A (Assumptions, Outcomes, Responses)
  - Scenario B ...
- **Visuals:**
  - 2x2 Scenario Matrix
  - Storyline Diagrams

## Use Cases
- **Business**: Long-term strategic planning and risk management.  
- **Daily Life**: Career and life path planning.  
- **Decision Making**: Making high-stakes decisions under significant uncertainty.  

## Common Misuses
- Creating too many scenarios, leading to **「analysis paralysis」**.  
- Ignoring plausibility or reality in the narratives.  
- Leaving the corresponding strategies or action plans ambiguous.

## Relationship with Other Models
- **Related**: Second-Order Thinking, Bayesian Thinking.  
- **Complementary**: Expected Value Thinking.  
- **Contrast**: Single-Point Forecasting (predicting only one outcome).
